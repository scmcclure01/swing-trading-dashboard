# Swing Trading Dashboard — UX Audit & Redesign Recommendations

**Date:** June 7, 2026
**Scope:** Streamlit dashboard (`app.py`, 3,248 lines), focus on UX/workflow + visual cleanup
**Flagship screen:** Macro & Permission tab (Tab 1)
**Goals stated:** (1) too much navigation/clicking, (2) the "so what" is buried, (3) looks dated/cluttered
**Platform appetite:** open to a rebuild; portfolio-grade polish wanted

---

## 1. The core diagnosis

Your dashboard does not have a *data* problem. It has a **hierarchy** problem.

The instinct when something "looks cluttered" is to remove data. That would be the wrong move here — the data is the point, and you're a sophisticated user who wants density. The benchmark you chose (information density done right) is exactly correct, and the literature backs why your current screen fails.

Matt Ström's framework on UI density separates three things:

- **Visual density** — how much is on screen. Yours is high. *This is fine.*
- **Design density** — whether Gestalt grouping (proximity, similarity, hierarchy) makes the structure legible. **Yours is broken.**
- **Value density** — whether the highest-value output is the easiest thing to extract. **Yours is broken.**

Bloomberg's Terminal is the canonical dense interface, and it works because every region is *visually differentiated* and the trader knows instantly where the answer lives. Your Macro tab does the opposite: eight cards, identical white background, identical border, identical heading style, identical table treatment, stacked in two equal columns. By the **principle of similarity**, your eye reads all eight as equally important — so none of them is. The conclusion has nowhere to land.

> **The one-line summary:** Your permission-state verdict — the entire output of the screen — is rendered as a thin green strip *above the tab bar*, smaller than any of the eight data tables that are supposed to merely *support* it. The evidence is louder than the verdict. That single inversion is the root of all three complaints.

## 2. What the screenshot and code confirm

### 2.1 The verdict is demoted, the evidence is promoted

In `main()`, the GREEN-state banner ("Full deployment. Momentum breakouts. Up to 20 positions...") renders as a single-line strip. Then the Macro & Permission tab opens with the **Sector Relative Strength table** as its first, top-left element. So the screen that answers *"what am I allowed to do this week?"* leads with a 10-row supporting table and hides the answer in a banner you scroll past.

This is a **value-density failure**: the most valuable output (the permission state and its rules) takes the most effort to extract.

### 2.2 Eight cards, zero hierarchy

`_render_layer0_2_tab()` builds a `grid-template-columns:1fr 1fr` with eight `_card()` calls, all identical treatment:

| Left column | Right column |
|---|---|
| Sector Relative Strength | SPY Two-Speed Trend |
| Velocity Flag — ROC 21 | Recession Composite |
| Bond & Liquidity | Layer 1 — Monthly Mispricing |
| Sector Flow Momentum | Gate Summary |

Nothing signals that **SPY Two-Speed + Gate Summary** are the gate-deciding inputs while **Sector Flow Momentum** is a "L3 Preview." They all look the same. A reader can't tell the load-bearing signals from the reference tables — a **design-density failure** (Gestalt: no differentiation = no grouping).

### 2.3 The header tiles are underused

Eight tiles run across the top — Regime, Permission, SPY, Net Positions, Core Deployed, Velocity Flag, Max Heat, Drawdown. These *are* your "so what" metrics, but `tile()` styles them flat and uniform: same subtle-blue background, same small label, same weight. They read as a footer, not a headline. This is the single highest-leverage area to fix — these tiles should *be* the answer to the screen.

### 2.4 Column imbalance / wasted space

The left column runs long (4 tables, including the 10-row Sector RS and 10-row Velocity Flag) while the right column's lower half leaves significant whitespace below the Gate Summary. The 1fr/1fr split doesn't match the actual content weight, so the screen feels both crowded (left) and empty (right) at once.

### 2.5 A real bug rendering to the user

The Fed Net Liquidity row shows **"The read operation timed out"** — a raw exception string surfaced as user-facing content (`l0.get("fnl_error", "N/A")` in the bond table). A failed data pull should degrade gracefully (e.g., "Fed Net Liquidity: unavailable — retry") not leak a Python timeout message. Small, but it's exactly the kind of thing that makes a tool read as "unpolished."

### 2.6 Maintainability (your cleanup goal)

`app.py` is **3,248 lines in a single file** — render functions, data plumbing, the screener (`run_screener_v3`, ~400 lines), charting, and portfolio logic all in one module. You already started extracting (`theme.py`, `ui_components.py`, `layers.py`, `data/`), but the main file is still a monolith. This isn't user-visible, but it's the reason every UX change feels heavy and risky. Cleanup here directly serves the velocity of every future design change.

## 3. Benchmark patterns worth stealing

From best-in-class finance UIs (Bloomberg Terminal, Koyfin) and the fintech-dashboard literature:

1. **Verdict-first, evidence-second.** Users should grasp the key insight in under 5 seconds. The conclusion gets the largest type and boldest weight; supporting metrics get smaller, lighter treatment. Visual weight = importance, not decoration. *(This is the fix for "so what is buried.")*

2. **Differentiate regions so density stays legible.** Bloomberg is denser than your dashboard, yet more navigable, because every zone looks distinct and the eye knows where each answer lives. Use Gestalt similarity *deliberately*: load-bearing signals get one visual treatment, reference tables get a quieter one.

3. **Every pixel accountable (Tufte's data-ink).** Maximize the ratio of information to chrome — but the goal is hierarchy, not deletion. Keep the data; reduce the redundant borders, repeated headings, and uniform card chrome that add ink without adding information.

4. **Card-based layout with intra-card mini-trends.** Modern finance dashboards embed sparklines/mini-charts inside metric cards so a trend is readable at a glance without a separate chart tab.

5. **Temporal density matters as much as visual.** A dense screen that loads slowly feels worse than a sparse one that's instant. Your yfinance/FRED pulls are the temporal-density lever — caching and graceful degradation (see the timeout bug) are part of "feeling polished," not just speed.

## 4. Recommendations — prioritized

### Tier 1 — Quick wins (within Streamlit, low risk, high impact)

1. **Promote the verdict to a hero block.** Move the permission-state banner *onto* the Macro tab as a full-width hero at the very top: large state word (GREEN), the rule it implies ("Up to 20 positions · 0.75–1.0% risk · Core 40%"), and the 2–3 gates that decided it. This alone resolves "so what is buried."
2. **Re-rank the header tiles by importance.** Make Permission + Regime the dominant tiles (larger, bolder); demote the rest to a secondary row. Use the existing `GATE` palette so the tile color *is* the state.
3. **Visually separate load-bearing vs. reference cards.** Give the gate-deciding cards (SPY Two-Speed, Gate Summary, Recession Composite) a stronger treatment (accent left-border or filled header); quiet down the previews (Sector Flow "L3 Preview," Velocity Flag) with a lighter background. Pure CSS in `ui_components.py`.
4. **Fix the Fed Net Liquidity error string.** Catch the timeout and render a clean "unavailable" state with a retry note.
5. **Rebalance the columns.** Move one card across, or switch to a content-weighted split (e.g., `1.1fr 0.9fr`) so the right column isn't half-empty.

### Tier 2 — Structural (still Streamlit, moderate effort)

6. **Add a "Today" summary strip** above the tabs (or as the hero): the single most-asked question — *what changed and what am I allowed to do* — answered before any table. Pulls the verdict from every layer into one place.
7. **Add mini-trend sparklines** to the header tiles (SPY 1M, Core deployment trend, drawdown) so each tile carries a glance-level trend, not just a number.
8. **Standardize empty/error/stale states.** You already have `_staleness()` — extend that pattern so every data-dependent card has a consistent "stale / unavailable / live" indicator instead of ad-hoc error strings.

### Tier 3 — Cleanup (invisible to users, enables everything above)

9. **Decompose `app.py`.** Extract the screener into `screener.py`, charting into `charts.py`, portfolio into `portfolio.py`, and the per-tab render functions into a `tabs/` package. Target: `app.py` becomes a thin orchestrator under ~300 lines. This de-risks every future UX change.
10. **Consolidate the duplicate `cb_table`.** There's a `cb_table` in both `app.py` (line 841) and `ui_components.py` — single source of truth.

### Tier 4 — Rebuild question (you said you're open to it)

You're open to a rebuild, so here's the honest verdict:

**Recommendation: stay on Streamlit for now, but extract your render layer to be framework-agnostic.**

- **Why not rebuild yet:** Streamlit's real ceiling is the page chrome (tab bar, spacing, the inability to do true fixed/sticky regions or fine-grained layout control). But you're a *single user running a weekly workflow*, not shipping a product. Tiers 1–3 recover ~80% of the polish you want without a rewrite, and Streamlit's superpower — Python data pulls wired straight into the UI — is exactly what your workflow needs.
- **When a rebuild pays off:** if you later want (a) a genuinely Bloomberg-like dense single-screen cockpit with sticky panels and keyboard navigation, (b) real-time/intra-day updates, or (c) to show it to others as a polished product. At that point a React/HTML front-end over a thin Python/FastAPI data API is the move — and because you'd already have a clean data layer from Tier 3, that rebuild becomes a front-end swap, not a ground-up rewrite.
- **The smart hedge:** Do Tier 3 (clean data/render separation) regardless. It improves Streamlit today *and* makes an eventual rebuild cheap. You don't have to decide now.
